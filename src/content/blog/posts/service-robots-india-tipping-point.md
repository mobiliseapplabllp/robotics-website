Technology markets tip when three curves cross: the **cost curve** of the technology falls, the **cost curve** of the alternative rises, and the **reliability curve** of the technology crosses an acceptability threshold.

For service robotics in India, all three curves crossed between 2022 and 2024. 2026 is the year the market responds.

## Curve one — hardware cost is down 55% in five years

In 2019, a KEENON DINERBOT T5 delivered to an Indian restaurant cost roughly what a mid-range car cost. In 2026, the comparable KEENON T8 costs what a high-end two-wheeler costs — and does more.

Three drivers flattened this curve:

- **LiDAR commoditisation** — once a ₹3 lakh component, now ₹30,000 with better resolution
- **Edge compute at BOM scale** — Rockchip/Allwinner SoCs delivering 1 TOPS at $20
- **Battery chemistry improvements** — LFP cells at half the 2019 price with 2× cycle life

KEENON, PuduTech, and Orion Star have all pushed five distinct hardware generations through this cost curve. Each generation is 20–30% cheaper and meaningfully better than the previous one.

## Curve two — Indian service wages have jumped 35–55% since 2019

This is the one Indian operators feel in their P&L every month.

A housekeeping attendant in a Gurgaon 5-star hotel earned ₹15,000–18,000 in 2019. The same role today, including mandatory statutory benefits and what you have to pay to retain them, is ₹28,000–35,000 — and the retention horizon shrunk from 2 years to 8 months.

A restaurant runner in a Mumbai casual-dining chain was ₹16,000 in 2019. Today: ₹26,000+ with wages climbing by the quarter and still unable to fill peak-hour shifts.

The maths of a delivery robot used to be: *replaces 0.8 FTE over 36 months, maybe break even.*
The maths today: *replaces 1.3–1.7 FTE over 18 months, with margin to spare on top.*

Wage inflation is a one-way ratchet. This curve only goes up.

## Curve three — reliability crossed the "boring technology" line

There's a moment in every technology's adoption where it stops being interesting and starts being infrastructure. Elevators in the 1920s. Desktop computers in the 1990s. Service robots are there now, or close to it.

A production-grade delivery robot in 2026:

- **Runs 22 hours a day** on an autonomous-charging cycle
- **Navigates dynamic environments** (people, carts, spills, open doors) using multi-sensor fusion
- **Handles 300–500 deliveries per day** with <0.5% delivery errors
- **Needs a software update roughly every 8 weeks** — not a crash every 8 hours
- **Integrates with lift-control systems** via standardised IoT protocols — cross-floor deliveries without human intervention

The robots that deployed into Singapore hotels in 2019 were experiments. The ones deploying into them today are appliances. Operators don't ask *"will it work?"* anymore — they ask *"what's my payback period?"*

## Why India specifically

Three things make India especially primed right now:

### 1. Post-COVID infrastructure investment

India's hotel, hospital, and airport construction boom since 2021 has **delivered robot-ready infrastructure at scale**. Modern lifts with IoT APIs. Wide corridors. Smooth-finish floors. Wi-Fi throughout. The hardware can't operate on 1990s infrastructure, and India spent the 2020s upgrading exactly the buildings these robots belong in.

### 2. Tourism + healthcare + retail converging on "hotel-grade service"

India's medical tourism market hit $13B in 2025. Tier-1 city malls aspire to Dubai Mall retail experience. Premium F&B chains are competing on ambience and speed, not just food. Every one of those competitive pressures rewards automation that delivers consistent, 24/7, no-sick-days service.

### 3. The labour arithmetic finally works across use cases

Different use cases tip at different cost points:

| Use case | Tipped when hardware cost fell below |
|---|---|
| Industrial cleaning (C40, C55) | ₹15 lakhs |
| Hotel room service (T9, W3) | ₹8 lakhs |
| Restaurant delivery (T8, T10) | ₹6 lakhs |
| Hospital logistics (T3, T11) | ₹10 lakhs |
| Corporate reception (S100, G-series) | ₹5 lakhs |

Every one of these price points is a 2024–2025 reality, not a 2028 projection.

## What we're watching

The dam will break unevenly. Our bet on sequencing:

- **2026**: Hospitality flagships (5-star hotels) and premium F&B chains go first — they're status-signalling buyers
- **2026–27**: Tertiary-care hospital networks follow, led by Apollo/Fortis/Manipal
- **2027**: Malls, airports, and large corporates — slower procurement cycles but bigger contracts
- **2027–28**: Tier-2 cities and mid-market hotels catch up

The first operators in each segment become the reference customers every subsequent buyer calls. Being among those first deployments isn't a branding flex — it's a competitive moat that lasts five years.
