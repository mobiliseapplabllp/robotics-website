Every sales conversation we have with hotels reaches the same five-minute mark: the GM nods at the demo, the F&B Director is asking about cabin hygiene, the IT Head is asking about Wi-Fi — and then the CFO (sometimes on speakerphone) cuts in with: *"What's the payback?"*

The honest answer is: it depends on your specific property. The useful answer is: here's how to model it for yours. This is the spreadsheet we'd build with you if you asked us on a Tuesday call.

## The canonical scenario

Let's model a 250-room 5-star hotel in Delhi-NCR. Three working assumptions:

- **80 room-service deliveries per day** on average (guest-room + pool-deck + club-lounge)
- **Current staffing**: 3 F&B runners across 3 shifts, each at ₹28,000/month fully loaded (wages + PF + ESI + meals + uniforms)
- **Current average delivery time**: 18 minutes (kitchen-to-room, per property GM self-report)
- **Target after automation**: 10 minutes, with 1.5 runners freed for guest-facing roles

## Cost side — the robot

A KEENON W3 ButlerBot (multi-compartment, lift-integrated, hygienic delivery robot) into an Indian hotel in 2026:

| Line item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Robot (CapEx or 36-month operating lease equivalent) | ₹11.5 lakhs |
| Installation + commissioning | ₹85,000 |
| Operator training (2 staff × 1 day) | ₹25,000 |
| Lift integration (IoT module + vendor coordination) | ₹60,000 |
| Annual maintenance contract (AMC) — yr 1 free, yr 2+ | ₹95,000/yr |
| Operating cost (electricity, consumables, parts reserve) | ₹35,000/yr |
| **Total Year-1** | **₹13.2 lakhs** |
| **Year-2 onward** | **₹1.3 lakhs/yr** |

## Savings side — the labour replaced

The robot doesn't replace 3 FTE; that's the sales-deck pitch, not reality. It displaces the most automatable portion of 3 jobs, which is roughly 1.5 FTE:

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Runners freed for guest-facing work | 1.5 FTE |
| Fully-loaded monthly cost (₹28K × 1.5) | ₹42,000/month |
| Annual savings | ₹5.04 lakhs |

We're being conservative on purpose. The remaining 1.5 FTE get redeployed to roles that actually *add* revenue — concierge, upselling at the club lounge, turndown service. Some GMs model those redeployments as positive revenue. We prefer to leave them as "free upside" and model only the direct cost reduction.

## Payback

Pure cash-on-cash payback, ignoring soft benefits:

- **Net Year-1 cost**: ₹13.2 lakhs
- **Year-1 savings**: ₹5.04 lakhs
- **Year-2 onward savings**: ₹5.04 lakhs − ₹1.3 lakhs = ₹3.74 lakhs/yr net
- **Simple payback**: 2 years, 9 months
- **5-year cash position**: +₹5 lakhs net

For a 5-star hotel that amortises capex over 7 years, this is an easy yes. The CFO's bar for a labour-saving capex is usually under 3 years, and you've cleared it with room to spare.

## The numbers you can add in (if you're ready to stretch)

The above is the floor. Most properties see one or more of these:

### 1. Guest satisfaction lift

A W3 delivers in 10 minutes. Guests notice. Post-stay surveys tracking "speed of service" lift 15–20 points in the first 90 days across every property we've benchmarked (KEENON global data; Indian validation in progress).

**Financial impact**: if your property has 40,000 occupied room-nights/year at ₹8,500 ADR, a 3% lift in guest retention rate is worth ~₹1 crore in lifetime revenue. Most properties won't model this. They should.

### 2. Consistency during peaks

The robot works at 2 AM on Diwali. It works during a wedding. It works when your third runner has called in sick and the banquet is already serving 340 covers.

The cost of a service failure at a luxury hotel isn't just the complaint — it's the post-stay review, the OTA rating, the lost repeat booking. Even one prevented incident/month pays a meaningful chunk of the AMC.

### 3. Staffing volatility insurance

Your current runner turnover is 40–60% annually. Each replacement costs ~₹25,000 in recruitment + ₹45,000 in lost productivity during ramp. The robot's "turnover rate" is zero. Your AMC covers it.

Annual saving on recruitment churn alone: ~₹2.1 lakhs for a 3-runner team.

### 4. Insurance against the next shock

COVID wasn't a 100-year event — it was a warning shot. The next labour shock (pandemic, regulatory change, mass migration event) will arrive. Operators with robotic logistics ride it out; operators without will staff-spiral.

This is harder to price but a growing number of ops heads do model it — we've seen figures from ₹2–10 lakhs/property/year depending on how conservative your "risk manager" voice is.

## The real question your CFO is asking

The question isn't "does the math work?" It's "does the math work enough that this is the best use of this ₹13 lakhs right now?"

Our honest answer — and the reason we can say this with a straight face — is: **it's in your top three capex options for this year if your property fits the profile.** The other two are usually an F&B refresh or an IT upgrade. Both are productive. Neither provides daily, compounding labour savings.

## How to actually decide

If you're a GM or VP Ops thinking about this, the three-step decision framework we recommend:

1. **Model your own numbers.** We'll send you the spreadsheet with your delivery volumes, wage structure, and property layout plugged in. It's free. We don't need a purchase commitment to share it.
2. **Run a 60-day pilot at your flagship.** One robot, one floor. Your team sees it in operation. We measure delivery time, guest NPS, staff feedback.
3. **Decide based on the pilot data, not the sales pitch.** If the numbers don't hit, we pull the robot out. If they do, scale to additional robots or properties.

No hotel GM has ever regretted running the pilot. Some have decided afterward that it's not right for them *yet* — that's a valid outcome. What hasn't happened is anyone piloting a W3 and being surprised by its limitations.

The math is the math. What's left is whether your property is ready to be operated by it.
